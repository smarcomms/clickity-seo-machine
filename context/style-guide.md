# Clickity Style Guide

This guide defines writing conventions, formatting standards, and editorial guidelines for all Clickity content.

Clickity is an AI-native social media management platform that pairs AI agents with human Brand Managers to turn content into revenue. The brand promise is **"smart automation with a human touch."** The voice is friendly, human, expressive, confident, and direct. It is never robotic. Copy that sounds machine-generated betrays the whole proposition, so this guide exists to keep our writing sounding like a sharp, helpful human, not marketing autopilot.

> **Source of truth.** This guide is derived from `context/product-context.md`. Where the two conflict, product-context.md wins. Read its Section 9 (brand identity), Section 10 (messaging do's and don'ts), and Section 11 (internal only) before writing.

---

## Hard rules (read these first)

These are non-negotiable and apply to every piece of customer-facing copy. They come straight from product-context.md §9 and §10.

1. **No em dashes. Ever.** Use commas, colons, parentheses, or restructure the sentence. Em-dash use is both a founder-level style rule and a top AI-writing tell. There is no "use sparingly" exception in our house style. The character `—` does not belong in Clickity copy.
2. **No corporate padding or filler.** Frank and clear beats polished and hollow. If a sentence survives deletion without losing meaning, delete it.
3. **Never sound robotic or like AI autopilot.** "Smart automation with a human touch" is the brand promise. Machine-sounding copy directly contradicts the product. Read every draft aloud; if it sounds like a press release or a LinkedIn motivational post, rewrite it.

Everything else in this guide supports these three rules.

## Language variant: American English (default)

**Default to American English:** *organize*, *optimize*, *color*, *center*, *behavior*, *-ize* not *-ise*, *program* (not *programme*).

**Why:** the target audience (product-context.md §3) is US small and mid-sized businesses, HVAC companies, real estate agents, local retailers competing on foot traffic, and US e-commerce brands. US spelling matches both the audience and US search behavior, so it is the correct reader-comfort and SEO call.

**Note on the company entity:** the company entity is UK-based, so internal, legal, or contractual documents may use British English. That does not change the default for customer-facing marketing copy, which is American English.

**RESOLVED (2026-06-22): American English is the confirmed default** for all customer-facing marketing copy (the audience skews US). The UK company entity uses British English only for internal/legal/contractual documents, per the note above.

**Consistency rule:** never mix variants within a single piece. Pick American English and hold it. Where a British-spelled term must appear verbatim (a proper noun, a legal entity name, an exact-match quote), keep it as-is and leave the surrounding prose American.

## Brand terms (use these exactly)

- **Clickity**: always capitalized, capital C, one word, always. Never "ClickIty," "clickity," "Clickity.io," or "Click-ity." The only exception is the logo wordmark, which is a locked lowercase custom logotype ("clickity") and must never be re-typed, re-fonted, or recreated in running text. In copy, you always write "Clickity."
- **Platform.** Clickity is an AI-native social media management platform. Refer to it as a platform.
- **Brand Manager**: capital B, capital M. This is the human point of contact and the strategic human layer. Never call them a "social media manager," "content creator," or "account manager." The term signals senior, judgment-driven, strategic scope, which is the premium customers pay for.
- **the Brief**: the living, compounding brand knowledge base (the structural differentiator). Capital B when referring to the product concept. It accumulates brand intelligence so quality improves the longer a customer stays.
- **Tagline:** **"Turn content into revenue."** Use it verbatim as the hero line. Do not paraphrase it ("turn content into sales," "content that converts," etc.). The internal framing "AI for outputs, humans for outcomes" is operating logic, not a customer slogan, so keep it out of public copy.
- **Domain:** useclickity.com. Ignore `clickity.io` and any placeholder contact details.

**RESOLVED (2026-06-22): "the Brief" is the public-facing name.** Use "the Brief" in all customer-facing copy for the brand knowledge base. "Project" is an internal engineering term only and must never appear in published content.

## Visual and brand mechanics

So any on-page styling, callouts, or embedded design stays on brand (product-context.md §9). Writers and anyone setting page styling should respect these.

- **Theme:** dark. Black canvas, white text, orange as the single accent.
- **Colors:**
  - Black `#000000` (primary background)
  - White `#FFFFFF` (text / reverse)
  - Orange `#FE6D08` (accent only)
- **Orange is a highlight, not a flood color.** Use it sparingly and intentionally for key words, CTAs, the icon, and accent details.
- **Typography:** a serif display for headings, with key accent words set in **italic and orange** (the signature treatment, e.g. "About *Clickity*"), and a clean sans-serif for body text, white on black.
- **Don't hand-set colors in body copy.** Use the site's default styling so links and accents stay consistent.

## Grammar and mechanics

### Capitalization

**Headings and subheadings:**
- **Sentence case.** Capitalize only the first word and proper nouns. ("How to turn a blog post into ten pieces of content," not "How To Turn A Blog Post Into Ten Pieces Of Content.")
- Title case reads corporate and dated. Sentence case reads human, which is the point.

**Industry terms** (lowercase unless starting a sentence):
- content marketing, social media, social media management, lead generation
- SEO / GEO / AEO: always uppercase
- AI: always uppercase ("AI agents," "AI-generated")
- LinkedIn, X, Reddit, Quora, Medium, Instagram, Facebook, Threads, Pinterest, TikTok, YouTube, Google Business Profile: match each platform's own capitalization

### Oxford comma

- **No Oxford comma** (default): "research, write and publish." Add the final comma only where omitting it creates genuine ambiguity. Hold this consistently across a piece.

### Em dashes

- **Banned.** See the Hard Rules. Use commas, colons, parentheses, or restructure. The em dash `—` does not appear in Clickity copy.

### Other punctuation

- **Quotation marks:** curly (smart) quotes in published prose: " " and ' '. Straight quotes are fine in code, file names, and raw URLs.
- **Ellipses:** three dots (… or `...`), no surrounding spaces. Use only for omitted text in quotes, never for manufactured suspense.

### Numbers

**When to spell out:**
- Spell out one through nine.
- Use numerals for 10 and above.
- Always numerals for: percentages (5%, 50%), money ($5, $500), measurements and stats (5x, 3 posts/week), and any number in a stat, headline, or list where scannability matters.

**Large numbers:**
- Use a comma as the thousands separator: 1,000+; 10,000 not 10000.
- Spell out million and billion: "1.2 million," "$3 million."
- Use $5K shorthand only in informal or social contexts, not formal article body copy.

**Currency:**
- Default to **$ (USD)** to match the US audience. Don't mix currencies in one piece without a clear reason (e.g. an explicit conversion).

### Abbreviations and acronyms

- Spell out on first use with the acronym in parentheses: "Answer Engine Optimization (AEO)."
- Skip the spell-out for universally known terms: SEO, AI, URL, CTA, ROI, B2B.
- **Latin abbreviations:** prefer plain English. "For example" not "e.g.," "that is" not "i.e.," "and so on" not "etc." The Latin forms are acceptable inside parenthetical asides where brevity helps.

**Common acronyms:**
- SEO: Search Engine Optimization
- GEO: Generative Engine Optimization
- AEO: Answer Engine Optimization
- CTA: Call to Action
- ROI: Return on Investment
- SERP: Search Engine Results Page

## Word choice and usage

### Preferred terms

**Say this** → **Not that:**
- platform → tool, app (when describing Clickity as a whole)
- AI agents with human Brand Managers → "fully automated," "AI does the work" (it erases the human layer)
- Brand Manager → social media manager, content creator, account manager
- turn content into revenue → "boost your brand," "increase engagement" (vague)
- grow / growth / outcomes → "level up," "supercharge," "10x your reach" (hype clichés)
- write / publish / distribute → "ideate," "amplify," "actualize"
- results / outcomes → "impact" used as a filler noun

### Words and phrases to avoid

These are filler and AI tells. They make copy sound robotic, which is the one thing we can't be.

- **synergy**
- **leverage** (as a verb or filler; "use" is almost always better)
- **utilize** (just say "use")
- **unlock**, **elevate** (as filler)
- **seamless** (don't overuse; it reads as a generic AI adjective)
- **delve**
- **"in today's fast-paced world"** (and cousins: "in the ever-evolving landscape of," "in the digital age," "now more than ever")
- **"it's not just X, it's Y"** (and its variants: "it's not about X, it's about Y," "this isn't just X, it's Y")
- Other generic AI vocabulary: robust, cutting-edge, best-in-class, game-changer, revolutionary, next-level, supercharge, turbocharge, tapestry, realm, testament, beacon, navigate, foster, harness, pivotal, multifaceted, underscore.

### The "AI slop" rule

There is one simple rule here, and it replaces any older "banned word" framing:

- **Don't write "AI slop" (or "slop") in customer-facing copy.** It's harsh and off-brand (product-context.md §10).
- You *may* position against the substance of generic, robotic, judgment-free content. The idea is exactly what Clickity competes against. Just describe it plainly ("generic," "off-brand," "templated," "content that ignores your data") rather than using the word "slop." Critique the substance, not with the slur.

### General weak words

- Cut "very," "really," "actually," "just," "literally." Almost always removable.
- Prefer active voice when it's clearer.
- Never write "click here" or "read more." Use descriptive link text.

### Inclusive language

- Use gender-neutral language; singular "they" is fine.
- Avoid region-locked idioms and sports metaphors that don't travel.
- Keep language accessible and plain-spoken, not jargon-heavy.

## AI-watermark and "sounds robotic" avoidance

This is the most important section in the guide after the Hard Rules. The brand promise is smart automation with a *human* touch. Content that reads as machine-generated breaks that promise. Strip every one of these tells before publishing.

**Hard bans (dead giveaways):**
- **The "it's not just X, it's Y" construction.** Banned outright, including all its cousins.
- **"In today's fast-paced world,"** "in the ever-evolving landscape of," "in the digital age," "now more than ever," "when it comes to."
- **Em dashes.** Already banned (see Hard Rules). AI loves stacking them; we use none.
- **The rule-of-three everywhere.** Constant triads ("clear, concise, and compelling") signal AI cadence. Vary sentence rhythm; break the triplet habit.
- **Empty throat-clearing openers:** "Let's dive in," "Let's explore," "Buckle up," "Picture this."
- **Over-hedging:** "it's important to note that," "it's worth mentioning," "needless to say."
- **Hollow conclusions:** "In conclusion," "At the end of the day," "The bottom line is."
- **Generic AI vocabulary:** delve, tapestry, realm, testament, beacon, navigate, foster, leverage, robust, seamless, elevate, unleash, harness, pivotal, multifaceted, underscore.
- **Bold-and-colon list intros** where every bullet starts with a bolded two-word phrase followed by a colon and a tidy clause. Vary the structure.

**Do this instead:**
- Open with a concrete claim, a number, a real example, or a blunt statement of the problem.
- Vary sentence length deliberately. Short sentences land. Then a longer one carries the reasoning.
- Use specific nouns and real examples over abstract summaries.
- Read it aloud. If it sounds like a press release or a LinkedIn motivational post, rewrite it.
- When in doubt, cut. Plain and specific beats smooth and empty every time.

## Formatting standards

### Text formatting

- **Bold:** for genuine key concepts and takeaways. If everything is bold, nothing is.
- **Italics:** sparingly, for light emphasis, titles of works, or a single highlighted word. (Note: orange italic accent words are a brand display treatment, not for body copy.)
- **Underline:** links only.
- **ALL CAPS:** avoid except for standard acronyms (SEO, AI, URL).

### Lists

- **Bulleted lists:** for non-sequential items. Capitalize the first word of each item. Full stop only if the item is a complete sentence; no full stop for fragments. Keep parallel structure.
- **Numbered lists:** for sequential steps, rankings, ordered priorities. Format `1.` `2.` `3.` (full stop after the number). Same capitalization and punctuation rules as bullets.
- **Nested lists:** maximum two levels deep.

### Links

- **Anchor text:** descriptive and specific, keyword-relevant where natural, typically 2 to 5 words. Yes: "how content distribution actually works." No: "click here," "read more."
- **Formatting:** use the site's default link color and styling. Don't hand-set colors in body copy. External links open in a new tab; internal links open in the same tab.

### Code and technical elements

- **Inline code:** backticks for URLs, file names, and code snippets.
- **Code blocks:** for multi-line code or configuration; include the language identifier for highlighting.

### Callout boxes / asides

- Use a Markdown blockquote (`>`) for pro tips, warnings, and key takeaways. Keep them short and earned, not a restatement of the paragraph above.

## Content structure

### Article introduction (150 to 250 words)

1. **Hook** (1 to 2 sentences): a blunt statement, a number, or the real problem. No throat-clearing.
2. **Problem** (2 to 3 sentences): the challenge the reader actually has.
3. **Promise** (2 to 3 sentences): what they'll be able to do after reading.
4. **Credibility** (optional, 1 to 2 sentences): why Clickity has standing to say this.

**Keyword placement:** include the primary keyword within the first 100 words, integrated naturally.

### Section length

- **Minimum:** 150 words per section.
- **Maximum:** 500 words per section (split if longer).
- **Ideal:** 250 to 350 words per main section.

### Conclusion (150 to 250 words)

1. **Recap:** the few things that actually matter.
2. **Action** (1 to 2 sentences): the next concrete step.
3. **CTA** (1 to 2 sentences): trial, consult, or related resource.
4. **Forward-looking** (optional, 1 sentence).
- Do not open with "In conclusion." Just conclude.

## SEO-specific style

### Meta titles
- 50 to 60 characters including spaces.
- Include the primary keyword.
- Include "| Clickity" if space allows.
- No ending punctuation.

### Meta descriptions
- 150 to 160 characters including spaces.
- Include the primary keyword and a call to action.
- End on a complete thought.

### URL slugs
- Lowercase only.
- Hyphens between words.
- Include the primary keyword.
- 3 to 5 words ideal.
- Format: `/blog/primary-keyword-phrase`.

### Alt text
- Describe what the image shows.
- Include the keyword naturally only if relevant.
- 125 characters or fewer.
- No "image of" / "picture of" (implied).

## Dates and time

- **Date format:** Month DD, YYYY (US standard): "January 15, 2025."
- **Time:** 12-hour with am/pm: "3:00pm." Use 24-hour only where a technical context demands it.
- **Time zones:** specify when relevant: "3:00pm ET." Default to US time zones for the US audience.

---

## Statistics and data

### Citing sources
- Always cite statistics with a named source.
- Format: "According to [Source], [statistic]."
- Link to the original source where possible.
- Include the year: "In 2025, …"
- Never invent or estimate figures. If you can't source it, don't claim it.

### Presenting numbers
- Round large numbers for readability: "1.2 million," not "1,234,567."
- Use the % symbol: 15% (not "percent").
- Use the currency symbol: $500 (not "dollars").
- Use commas in thousands: 10,000 not 10000.

---

## Images and media

- **Captions:** optional; add only when they add context the image can't carry alone. Sentence case; full stop if a complete sentence; placed below the image.
- **Screenshots:** include when they genuinely help. Crop to the relevant portion; add arrows or highlights. Alt text describes what's shown.
- **Charts and graphs:** use when visualization aids understanding. Keep the design simple and on-brand (dark theme, orange accent). Alt text describes the key finding, not just "chart."

## Accessibility

- Descriptive link text (never "click here").
- Alt text on every image.
- Proper heading hierarchy (H1 → H2 → H3, no skipping).
- Write at an 8th to 10th grade reading level.
- Define technical terms on first use.
- Short sentences, short paragraphs. Plain-spoken is the brand, not a compromise.

## Voice and tone reminders

### Core voice characteristics
1. Friendly and human: warm, expressive, approachable.
2. Confident and direct: opinionated and specific, never stiff, never insulting the reader.
3. Outcome-obsessed: tie content back to revenue and real business results (foot traffic, conversions, sales).
4. Anti-filler: cut the clichés and the corporate padding.
5. Human first: AI agents do the execution, but human judgment is the product. Smart automation with a human touch.

### Tone variations
- **How-to content:** direct, step-by-step, no padding.
- **Strategic content:** opinionated, experienced, actionable.
- **Industry / AI commentary:** insightful, a little contrarian, forward-looking.
- **Service / product content:** benefit-focused, honest, specific about outcomes.

## Internal only (never publish)

Per product-context.md §11, the following must never appear, paraphrased or direct, in any customer-facing asset:

- The Smarcomms-to-Clickity transition story or any "business turnaround" narrative.
- Headcount of any kind (prior team size, number of content creators, number of Brand Managers, upskilling or transitioning staff).
- Internal financials: margins, unit economics, churn, ARR targets. (The "$99/mo" entry price itself is fine to use publicly; the *narrative* behind it is not.)
- Raw competitive-analysis notes or named lists of incumbent tools being replaced.

Treat this as context that informs tone and conviction, never as source text.

## Editing checklist

Before publishing any content:

**Hard rules:**
- [ ] No em dashes anywhere
- [ ] No corporate padding or filler
- [ ] Reads like a human, not AI autopilot (read it aloud)

**Grammar and mechanics:**
- [ ] American English throughout (UK entity docs excepted)
- [ ] Grammar and punctuation checked (no Oxford comma unless needed)
- [ ] Numbers and currency ($) formatted consistently
- [ ] Dates in Month DD, YYYY format

**Brand terms:**
- [ ] "Clickity" spelled correctly; referred to as a platform
- [ ] "Brand Manager" (not social media manager); "the Brief" used correctly
- [ ] Tagline "Turn content into revenue" used verbatim if present
- [ ] No "AI slop" / "slop" in the copy

**AI-watermark sweep:**
- [ ] No "it's not just X, it's Y" constructions
- [ ] No "in today's fast-paced world" / clichéd openers
- [ ] No generic AI vocabulary (delve, leverage, robust, seamless, elevate, unlock…)
- [ ] Sentence rhythm varied; no relentless rule-of-three

**Structure and SEO:**
- [ ] Strong introduction (hook, problem, promise) with no throat-clearing
- [ ] Sentence-case headings; proper heading hierarchy
- [ ] Effective conclusion with CTA (no "in conclusion")
- [ ] Keyword integrated naturally; meta title and description optimized
- [ ] Links descriptive; images have alt text; URL slug optimized

**Quality and safety:**
- [ ] Factually accurate; no invented stats; sources cited
- [ ] No internal-only material (transition story, headcount, financials)
- [ ] Provides genuine value; ready for publication
